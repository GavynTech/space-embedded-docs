# Thermal Vacuum Environment

Space is not uniformly cold. A spacecraft in LEO cycles between +120°C in direct sunlight and -90°C in eclipse, every 95 minutes, for years. Managing temperature is a core engineering discipline — get it wrong and your battery dies, your electronics clock wrong, or your structure warps.

---

## Thermal Environment

### Heat Sources

| Source | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Solar radiation | 1361 W/m² (solar constant at 1 AU) |
| Albedo (Earth reflected) | ~30% of solar, varies with cloud cover |
| Earth IR | ~237 W/m² from Earth's thermal emission |
| Internal dissipation | Heat from electronics, motors, batteries |

### Heat Removal

There is no convection in vacuum — only radiation. The Stefan-Boltzmann law governs:

```
P_radiated = ε × σ × A × T⁴

where:
  ε = emissivity (0–1)
  σ = 5.67×10⁻⁸ W/m²·K⁴
  A = radiating area
  T = temperature in Kelvin
```

## Temperature Ranges

| Component | Operating Range | Survival Range |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Li-Ion Battery | 0°C to 45°C | -20°C to 60°C |
| Electronics (commercial) | 0°C to 70°C | -55°C to 85°C |
| Electronics (military) | -55°C to 125°C | -65°C to 150°C |
| Reaction wheels | -20°C to 50°C | -40°C to 60°C |
| Propellant (hydrazine) | > 2°C | > 2°C (freezes!) |

## Passive Thermal Control

### Multi-Layer Insulation (MLI)

Blankets of aluminized Mylar with low-emissivity spacers. Reduces radiative heat exchange with environment by 100×. Wraps most of the spacecraft body.

### Surface Coatings

| Surface | α (solar absorptivity) | ε (IR emissivity) | Use |
|---------|----------------------|--------------------|-----|
| Polished aluminum | 0.09 | 0.03 | Minimize heat loss |
| White paint | 0.20 | 0.90 | Radiator surface |
| Black paint | 0.95 | 0.90 | Absorb solar heat |
| Optical Solar Reflector (OSR) | 0.08 | 0.80 | High-power radiators |

### Radiators

Panels coated with high-emissivity paint, pointed away from the Sun, that dump waste heat to deep space.

### Heat Pipes

Passive two-phase heat transport — liquid evaporates at hot end, condenses at cold end, liquid returns via capillary wick. Copper-water for most applications; aluminum-ammonia for cryogenic or wide temperature ranges.

## Active Thermal Control

### Heaters

Resistive heaters keep cold components warm during eclipse. Thermostat-controlled or software-controlled:

```c
void thermal_heater_control(void) {
    float temp = read_thermistor(BATT_THERMISTOR);
    if (temp < BATT_HEATER_ON_THRESH) {
        eps_set_load(LOAD_BATT_HEATER, true);
    } else if (temp > BATT_HEATER_OFF_THRESH) {
        eps_set_load(LOAD_BATT_HEATER, false);
    }
}
```

Heaters are the largest non-payload power consumer during eclipse in many missions.

### Louvers

Bimetal-actuated venetian blind louvers over radiators. Open when hot, close when cold. Entirely passive, no power required.

### Thermoelectric Coolers (TECs)

Peltier devices for precise thermal control of detectors. High power, used only when detector noise sensitivity demands it.

## Thermal Vacuum Testing (TVT)

All spacecraft must survive thermal vacuum testing before launch:

1. **Thermal Balance Test**: validate thermal model against measured temperatures in steady-state hot/cold conditions
2. **Thermal Cycling**: cycle between hot and cold case multiple times to stress interfaces and connections
3. **Operational TVT**: run all subsystems at temperature, verify performance

Typical chamber conditions:
- Pressure: < 10⁻⁵ Torr (< 1.3×10⁻³ Pa)
- Hot case: +80°C environment
- Cold case: -40°C environment
- Cycles: 8–12 full hot/cold cycles

## Software Implications

Thermal state affects software directly:

- Crystal oscillators change frequency with temperature → time drift
- Flash write speed varies ±30% across temperature range
- Battery capacity drops at low temperature
- Some sensors have temperature-dependent calibration

Flight software must monitor thermal state and adjust timing-sensitive operations accordingly.
